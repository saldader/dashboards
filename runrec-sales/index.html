Sell the Excursion Birthday

The switch phrase · use this when the call gets price-heavy

Switch phrase
"Before I get into the math — can I tell you what the day actually looks like? Because when you picture your kid on the court, the price question answers itself."
Why this works: Most parents call asking "how much" because that's the easy question. They don't actually know what they're buying yet. The switch phrase gives you permission to slow down and paint the picture before defending a number.

The 5 emotional buttons (in closing order)

A birthday parent isn't buying court time. They're buying these. Push these in this order on the call.

1 · Kid's joy — primary closer

For a sports kid, this is the birthday they've been wanting. Most parties are kids burning energy on shared trampolines. Here, they're playing their sport. They're the star.

"For a sports kid, how many times has he had a party where he actually got to play his sport? Here, that's the whole thing."
2 · Parent hero — "I nailed this"

The parent doesn't just want their kid to be happy. They want to LOOK like they pulled off the perfect party. RunRec gives them that win without the work.

"You show up looking like you nailed it. The other parents are going to ask where you found this place."
3 · Parent ease — "You literally do nothing"

This is the biggest pain point for birthday-throwing parents — and it's our biggest weapon. You pay. You bring your kid. That's it.

"Once you book, we send you an RSVP link. You forward it to the parents. They click yes. Done. No group texts, no chasing people for headcounts, no managing the guest list. Two coordinators on every package run the entire party — games, transitions, the birthday song, cleanup. Every kid leaves with a gift. The birthday kid gets a special one. It's all on us. You're literally just there to enjoy it. Most parents tell us it's the most relaxed birthday party they've ever thrown."
RSVP feature is being built into the website now. Tell parents it's coming. When live, the link arrives the moment they book.
4 · Real sport — "This is the real thing"

This is the anti-Urban-Air positioning. Not a kids' party with games on the side. A real basketball / volleyball / dodgeball day, with a coordinator running plays.

"Pro-spec hardwood. The same surface the NBA plays on. His day, his court, his game."
5 · The photo — "The fridge picture"

Modern parents aren't just hosting parties — they're capturing memories. The visual at RunRec (15 kids on a full court, action mid-game, lights on the scoreboard) is unlike anything else.

"Can you picture the photo? Your kid's whole team on the court, games running, everyone going nuts. That's the picture that ends up on the fridge."

Pricing objections · NO math defense

When price comes up, the move is to redirect to the kid, the experience, and end with a question about the child. The parent loves their kid more than they love the math.

Objection · "$500 is a lot."
❌ DON'T say: "Well if you divide $499 by 15 kids, that's only $33 per kid..."

✅ Say:

Pricing reframe
"I hear you. What makes it worth it is the experience your kid gets — a full facility to themselves, a coordinator running games the whole time, real basketball on a real court with their friends. For a kid who loves sports, there's nothing like it in Waterloo. Most parents who come in say it's the best birthday their kid has ever had. What age is [child's name] turning?"
End with a question about the child. Moves the conversation from price to the person the party is for.
Objection · "Why is it more expensive than [other place]?"
❌ DON'T say: "Well, they include food and we don't, but if you add that up..."

✅ Say:

Experience vs experience
"Fair question. The difference isn't really about the price — it's about what the kids actually do. At most party venues, kids are burning energy. At RunRec, they're actually playing — real games, real competition, a coordinator calling plays the whole time. For a kid who's into basketball, it's a completely different day. "Totally get it. Can I ask — is it the price that feels uncertain, or is it more about whether this is the right fit for [child]? Forces them to name the real objection. Either way you've opened the right door.

The "what makes Urban Air different" learning

From the research:

The locker concept

We don't sell gym memberships. We sell lockers. A locker is a personal bank of pre-paid court time + priority booking + community perks. It's stored value. Lead with this concept on every membership pitch.

The Birthday → Membership Pivot Cross-Track

When a birthday call signals a membership opportunity. The data shows: this works rarely, fails often, and depends entirely on timing.

Hard rule: Don't pivot to membership during a birthday close. Wait until the birthday is booked OR the prospect explicitly signals recurring use.

The 4 patterns from the data

✓ WIN · Daniel/Alicia (May 6)

Setup: Outbound birthday renewal. Birthday already past — they were on vacation. Alicia volunteers a NEW need: training bookings.

Alicia: "We are actually thinking of doing some training at your location there."
Izzy [adapts in 4 sec]: "There's actually gonna be an email going out to all current and ex players about new promotions..."
Alicia: "We will be using your facility often in the next few weeks for sure."

Why it worked: Customer surfaced the need. Izzy didn't push. Owner credibility late, not early.

✓ PARTIAL WIN · Brie (Mar 27)

Setup: First-time inbound birthday. Brie's daughter wants free play, considering stripping the package.

Izzy: "After the party, you're obviously welcome to book really casual sessions... You can book a training court for $40 and bring a couple friends, just hang out."
Brie: "Yep. Yeah. Exactly."

Why it worked: Casual-booking pivot, NOT membership pitch. $40 training court hook. Bridge to repeat use without the year commitment.

✗ FAIL · Anna (DIY veteran)

Setup: 3–4 years of casual basketball + pizza without paying for the package. Outbound birthday renewal.

Why it failed: She's already extracting "birthday" value from a casual booking. Polite decline. Pushed too hard, killed the warm relationship.

Lesson: Veteran DIY customers don't convert to membership. Don't force it.

⚠ MIXED · Leanne (May 5)

Setup: Repeat customer (multiple bookings + family events). Teacher = network of kids. High LTV indicators.

Izzy: "We have a free program for middle schoolers... I'll get into it after. Sorry to derail here."

Diagnosis: Pivot CAN work because she's a connector. But buried at end-of-call and verbally hedged. Timing was sloppy.

Lesson: Teachers / coaches / community connectors are right pivot targets. Make the pivot intentional, not a side note.

The pivot decision tree

Don't pivot if:

  • It's a birthday close in progress (close the birthday first)
  • They're a one-off party booker (no recurring intent signaled)
  • They're a DIY veteran already extracting value casually
  • They haven't asked about pricing or recurring options

Do pivot if:

  • They explicitly say "weekly," "every Tuesday," "consistent group"
  • They volunteer a recurring use case (training, team practice)
  • They ask about hourly rates AFTER booking the birthday
  • They're a teacher / coach / youth pastor / community connector

How to pivot:

  1. Wait until the birthday is closed/booked
  2. Plant the seed: "By the way — for groups that come in regularly, we have a membership that ends up cheaper. Want me to send a one-pager?"
  3. Don't run the full membership pitch on the same call — schedule a follow-up
  4. If they say yes to the seed, run the 4-Q qualifier on the next call
