Inbound Parent Call Birthday

Parent calls about a birthday. Highest-volume sales motion. Real script structure built from Sahar (959s), Brie (567s), Pavin family arc, Leanne (745s).

1. Opener 2. Discovery 3. Value pitch 4. Tier-fit 5. Objections 6. Close

1 · Opener (10–15s)

Keep what works. Don't pre-qualify — let the parent lead.

Opener
"Hi there. It's RunRec. How can I help you?"

2 · Discovery (60–90s)

After the parent explains, ask in this order. Do NOT ask budget. The package selection reveals it.

  1. Name — "And your name was?"
  2. Familiarity — "Have you been to RunRec before, or do you know anyone who has?" essential — changes everything
  3. Date / time slot — "When were you looking?"
  4. Birthday kid age — "How old is the birthday kid?"
  5. Total kids + age mix — "How many kids, all one age or mixed?"
  6. Active vs hangers-on — "Plus younger siblings or older cousins?"
  7. Sport affinity — "Is there a sport they love, or whatever's fun?"

3 · Bundled-value pitch (180–240s)

Lead with the reframe. Verbatim Izzy:

"The party price isn't about the court time. It's about everything that comes with it."— Izzy, repeated across every long inbound call

Then list (in this order — what closed in real calls):

Bundled-value list
1. Coordinator(s) — "think of them as gym teachers running the day" (HERO has 1, SUPER HERO/FUN add a 2nd for 20+ kids) 2. Exclusive use — "all three courts, no other groups will be there" 3. Equipment — basketballs, dodgeball, floor hockey, soccer, jump ropes, cornhole 4. Adjustments — "lower the basketball hoops because they're younger" 5. Scoreboard — "they can use the scoreboard, be a referee" 6. Party space — tables, custom-logo tablecloths, photo backdrop 7. Food permission — "you can bring pizza, cake — only with the package" 8. Goodie bags + birthday gift — "everybody walks away with a gift bag" 9. Cleanup — "they're also the cleanup crew" 10. Music — Bluetooth, free Wi-Fi End with: "We do parties every weekend. We've done a billion of these."

4 · Tier-fit recommendation (60s)

Pre-empt the "I'm so confused" moment. Sahar literally said this on a 959s call. Don't make parents figure out which tier — STATE it.
Tier statement
"Based on what you told me — [X] active kids, age [Y], [date] — you'd be in our SUPER HERO tier at $599. Here's why: [12+ kids needs the upgraded package, OR the goodie bags matter for that age, OR the extra activities are worth it]."

5 · Objections (90–180s)

Memorized responses for the top 4. Full 12 in Objections.

  • Pricing pushback — bend within 3-kid range, NOT a discount
  • "Can we just rent the space?" — concrete math: $179/hr × 2 = $358 vs package value
  • "I have to talk to my husband" — Saturday afternoon books up 2–3 weeks out
  • "What about decorations / balloons?" — we provide tables, tablecloths, photo backdrop

6 · Close (60s)

Close sequence
1. Date close: "What date are you thinking?" [check calendar live — actually look at it] "Okay — that's open. Quick reality check on the surrounding weekends though: [name dates that are booked]. So if [date] is your target, today or tomorrow is when to lock it in." 2. Saturday context: "Saturday afternoons book 2-3 weeks out — that's just our pattern." 3. Direct to website: "therunrec.com → Birthdays → pick your tier → pick your slot." 4. Pre-call promise: "We'll call you 3-4 days before to nail down details." 5. Shoes reminder: "Only thing on you: every kid needs indoor and outdoor shoes."

The stripping defense (verbatim from Sahar call)

When they say "can I just rent the space?" — do the math live.
"Rent only" defense
"You could. Two hours of three courts is $179/hr × 2 = $358. But you'd lose: - The coordinator - Food permission - All the equipment - Ability to lower the hoops - The scoreboard For ~$140 more you're getting a service worth way more than that. Plus you have to bring everything yourself — and clean up at the end."

The restaurant-party anchor

Restaurant anchor
"A private room at a restaurant for 15 kids — the food bill, the tables, managing the space — you're probably at $600–$800. And the kids are sitting at a table for two hours. Here, they're running, playing, burning it out. Way better experience for the same money."
What NOT to compare against: Don't lead with Chuck E Cheese / Sky Zone unless the parent mentions them first. They're cheaper venues — comparing makes RunRec look expensive. Restaurant party comparison wins because the dollar amounts match.

The locker concept

We don't sell gym memberships. We sell lockers. A locker is a personal bank of pre-paid court time + priority booking + community perks. It's stored value. Lead with this concept on every membership pitch.

Membership prospects come from 3 places

  1. Booking-history outbound (most likely) — spot a customer who's booked 3+ times in 8 weeks. Call them. → Script
  2. Inbound inquiry (rare) — someone calls asking specifically about memberships
  3. Birthday → membership pivot — during a birthday call they signal recurring use. Don't pitch immediately. → The Pivot
Before any pitch — run the qualifier. 4 questions, 30 seconds. The Damien fix. → Open Qualifier

Booking-History Outbound Membership

When you spot someone who's booked multiple times in the last 4–8 weeks → call them → "you'd save money on a membership." The primary membership-acquisition path.

Trigger criteria

Customer has booked the facility 3+ times in the last 8 weeks. They're using us already — converting to a member is pure margin.

Pre-call prep · 1 min

  1. Pull their booking history — sessions, court size, time slots, group size
  2. Calculate: at hourly rate, how much have they paid?
  3. Compute: which tier fits them?
  4. Mentally run the qualifier

The script

Booking outbound
"Hey [Name] — it's Izzy from RunRec. How's everything going? Listen — I was looking at your bookings the other day. You've been in [X] times in the last [Y] weeks, which is a lot. We love seeing you here. The reason I'm calling: based on how often you're coming, you'd actually save money on a membership rather than booking every time. Got 5 minutes? I'd love to walk you through what makes sense for someone using us as much as you are."

If they say yes

  1. Run the 4-Q qualifier first (don't skip — this is the Damien fix)
  2. Then run the All-Star pitch (default tier)
  3. Frame the 12-month term yourself before they ask
  4. Send the link WHILE on the call — never "I'll send it later"

The All-Star pitch

All-Star pitch
"Based on what you've told me — group of [X], [Y] times a month — the All-Star locker is your fit. You get 18 hours loaded into your locker today — your bank, use it however. You're booking 8 weeks ahead of the public. Right now I can lock in your Fridays for the next 2 months before anyone else sees them. Once your hours run out, you keep booking at 10% off for the rest of the year. All in: $139 a month. For your group, that's less than $5 per person per session. Cheaper than coffee. You're already coming in regularly — the math says this is the cheaper option. Want me to walk you through getting locked in?"

If they push back

Believe them. Don't push. Plant seed and exit warm:

Soft exit
"Totally fair — you're already a regular. Just wanted to make sure you knew the membership existed because at your rate, you'd be saving real money. If anything changes, you've got my direct number. Keep coming."

4-Question Qualifier Membership

Run BEFORE any membership pitch. 30 seconds. This exists because of the Damien refund — we sold him a membership that didn't fit, he refunded.

Hard rule: if any answer disqualifies, do NOT pitch membership. Plant the seed and exit.
Q1 · Court type
Q1
"Are you usually booking the full court or the training court?"

Full court → ✅ proceed · Training court → ❌ STOP, do not pitch

Q2 · Group size
Q2
"How many people in your typical session — solo, 2–3, group of 4–8, or larger?"

1–3 → Core ($89) · 4–8 → All-Star ($139, default) · 10+ → Legend ($359)

Q3 · Frequency
Q3
"How often per month — once, weekly, multiple times a week?"

1×/mo → ❌ doesn't pay back · 2×/mo → ✅ Core · Weekly → ✅ All-Star/Legend

Q4 · Use case
Q4
"What are you using it for — pickup play, training, league, drills?"

Pickup/league → ✅ · Training/drills → ⚠️ likely training court — re-check Q1

The Damien case: He signed up for membership expecting it to apply to the training court. Found out it's full-court only. Refunded. The qualifier exists so this never happens again.

The Birthday → Membership Pivot Cross-Track

When a birthday call signals a membership opportunity. The data shows: this works rarely, fails often, and depends entirely on timing.

Hard rule: Don't pivot to membership during a birthday close. Wait until the birthday is booked OR the prospect explicitly signals recurring use.

The 4 patterns from the data

✓ WIN · Daniel/Alicia (May 6)

Setup: Outbound birthday renewal. Birthday already past — they were on vacation. Alicia volunteers a NEW need: training bookings.

Alicia: "We are actually thinking of doing some training at your location there."
Izzy [adapts in 4 sec]: "There's actually gonna be an email going out to all current and ex players about new promotions..."
Alicia: "We will be using your facility often in the next few weeks for sure."

Why it worked: Customer surfaced the need. Izzy didn't push. Owner credibility late, not early.

✓ PARTIAL WIN · Brie (Mar 27)

Setup: First-time inbound birthday. Brie's daughter wants free play, considering stripping the package.

Izzy: "After the party, you're obviously welcome to book really casual sessions... You can book a training court for $40 and bring a couple friends, just hang out."
Brie: "Yep. Yeah. Exactly."

Why it worked: Casual-booking pivot, NOT membership pitch. $40 training court hook. Bridge to repeat use without the year commitment.

✗ FAIL · Anna (DIY veteran)

Setup: 3–4 years of casual basketball + pizza without paying for the package. Outbound birthday renewal.

Why it failed: She's already extracting "birthday" value from a casual booking. Polite decline. Pushed too hard, killed the warm relationship.

Lesson: Veteran DIY customers don't convert to membership. Don't force it.

⚠ MIXED · Leanne (May 5)

Setup: Repeat customer (multiple bookings + family events). Teacher = network of kids. High LTV indicators.

Izzy: "We have a free program for middle schoolers... I'll get into it after. Sorry to derail here."

Diagnosis: Pivot CAN work because she's a connector. But buried at end-of-call and verbally hedged. Timing was sloppy.

Lesson: Teachers / coaches / community connectors are right pivot targets. Make the pivot intentional, not a side note.

The pivot decision tree

Don't pivot if:

  • It's a birthday close in progress (close the birthday first)
  • They're a one-off party booker (no recurring intent signaled)
  • They're a DIY veteran already extracting value casually
  • They haven't asked about pricing or recurring options

Do pivot if:

  • They explicitly say "weekly," "every Tuesday," "consistent group"
  • They volunteer a recurring use case (training, team practice)
  • They ask about hourly rates AFTER booking the birthday
  • They're a teacher / coach / youth pastor / community connector

How to pivot:

  1. Wait until the birthday is closed/booked
  2. Plant the seed: "By the way — for groups that come in regularly, we have a membership that ends up cheaper. Want me to send a one-pager?"
  3. Don't run the full membership pitch on the same call — schedule a follow-up
  4. If they say yes to the seed, run the 4-Q qualifier on the next call
